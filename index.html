About

I am a Visiting Research Student at the Tsung-Dao Lee Institute (Shanghai Jiao Tong University), working on ultra-high-energy gamma-ray astrophysics with LHAASO alongside Dr. Gwenael Giacinti and Dr. Hao Zhou.

I completed an integrated MPhys in Theoretical Physics at the University of St Andrews (2025) under Dr. Martin Dominik, and was a Gruber-funded Research Student at the European Southern Observatory working with Dr. Pooneh Nazari. I have also carried out RAS-funded research supervised by Dr. Indranil Banik.

My broader interests include γ-ray astronomy, protostellar systems, cosmology, and gravitational microlensing.

Research

Interests

My research interests span a wide array of subdisciplines in astronomy. First, I am currently studying protostellar systems, and how structure and evolution can bias chemical abundance measurments. In addition, I am interested in the Hubble Tension and the viability of various proposed solutions to alleviate it. Furthermore, I am actively working on explaining the observed ultra-high diffuse γ-ray excess that has been recently observed. Last but not least, I am finilising my master's thesis on using gravitational microlensing data alongside numerical and machine learning techniques to probe stellar atmospheres.

Chemistry of Protostellar Systems

In my research on the chemistry of protostellar systems at the European Southern Observatory with Dr. Pooneh Nazari, I focus on understanding recent mismatches between gas and ice abundance ratios reported by the JOYS survey. I use radiative transfer modelling to test whether physical structure alone can reproduce these observations or whether more complex chemistry is required. I find that structural effects can drive order of magnitude shifts in the inferred column density ratios, meaning geometry and disk or envelope conditions can strongly bias what we interpret as chemical differences.

The Local Void and Late-Time Modification Sollutions to the Hubble Tension in Cosmology

In a study with Indranil Banik, funded by a Royal Astronomical Society summer student grant, I tested whether a large local underdensity (a “local void”) can explain the data, and we found that void models fit a 20-year compilation of BAO measurements markedly better than a homogeneous Planck-based model, reducing the overall tension to roughly the 1σ level. In a separate project, I co-authored an analysis of modified gravity models in symmetric teleparallel f(Q) gravity, showing that some theoretically motivated forms struggle, while an exponential-type model can ease the Hubble tension but still runs into mild BAO consistency issues, highlighting how hard it is to satisfy all datasets at once. Finally, I have also contributed as a co-author to the CosmoVerse White Paper, which synthesizes the major observational tensions in cosmology, maps likely systematics, and outlines promising new-physics and analysis directions for the coming decade.

Explaining the Ultra-High Diffuse γ-ray Excess

I am currently engaged in research concerning the ultra-high energy diffuse γ-ray excess observed in the galactic plane by LHAASO. This is being done at TDLI (SJTU) in Shanghai, China. Under the guidance of Dr. Gwenael Giacinti and Dr. Hao Zhou, I aim to investigate what portion of the observed excess can be contributed to unresolved sources, such as pulsars, supernova remnants and γ-ray binaries. I aim to model the population and galactic distributiuon of these objects, as well as their propagation in reaching us. Fianlly, I will tune the results to the instrumentation response and masking of LHAASO, creating HEALpix skymaps, to directly compare my computational models with observations.

Probing Stellar Atmospheres Using Microlensing Caustic Crossings

My master's thesis, which was supervised by Dr. Martin Dominik, focused on recovering non-parametric limb darkening profiles using caustic crossings, which has never been done for fold caustics. This was done using complex numerical techniques such as the finite element method, and the product integral method to invert the Fredholm integral that connects the underlying stellar brightness profile to the flux observed during a microlensing event. I am currently working on publishing the results, and have already extended the work to include machine learning inversion techniques that outclass the afforementioned methods significantly.

Using machine learning to distinguish signal from spurious background noise in the XENONnT detector

During my time at Purdue University, I worked with my classmate Divij Agarwal and the Purdue XENONnT group to build a machine-learning classifier to separate signal from noise. With guidance from Dr. Husheng Guan, I developed, tuned, and evaluated several algorithms, comparing their performance on event classification. This was enabled by the fact that nuclear recoils from neutron scattering closely mimic those expected from WIMP interactions, allowing neutrons to serve as a calibration proxy. We calibrated the detector using a yttrium-beryllium neutron source that emits neutrons at 152 keV.
